An inverted index is a key data structure in information retrieval systems.
It maps each term to the list of documents where that term appears.
This allows fast searching and efficient query processing.
Building an inverted index involves tokenizing each document and removing stop words.
Stop words are excluded to save space and improve retrieval accuracy.
Stemming is often applied to reduce words to their root form for better matching.
Inverted indexes are widely used in modern search engines and digital libraries.
Each term in the index points to one or more document identifiers.
Advanced systems use compression techniques to save storage and speed up search.
Query processing uses the inverted index to quickly find relevant documents.
Boolean, phrase, and proximity queries depend on the inverted index structure.
Updating the index efficiently is important when new documents are added.
Ranking algorithms use term frequency and inverse document frequency to score results.
Inverted indexing has made large-scale document retrieval fast and practical.
Efficient indexing directly improves the performance of an IR system.
